Section 01

The Core Philosophy

The COMPASS framework integrates two fundamentally different kinds of athlete health data — each answering a different clinical question that the other cannot. Its architecture is grounded in the theoretical argument by Kalkhoven, Watsford & Impellizzeri (2021) that systemic training load metrics cannot, by themselves, provide a quantitative indication of tissue-level injury risk.

⌚ Readiness Score via Spike · Daily

  • Collected daily from Spike wearable
  • Daily averages: avg HRV, resting HR, sleep quality, ACWR
  • Baseline-adjusted by Spike before delivery
  • Delivered as a single 0–100 readiness score

Answers: "Is this athlete's body ready to perform?"

Spike collects raw sensor data continuously and publishes one daily summary per athlete — end-of-day averages for HRV, resting HR, sleep score, and ACWR. These daily statistics are what flow into the readiness score delivered to COMPASS. Baseline establishment and longitudinal normalisation are handled entirely by Spike. COMPASS consumes the output score — no internal wearable baseline is required.

VS

🌡️ Thermography via NUR sensor

  • Contralateral asymmetry index
  • Asymmetry trend over time
  • Focal vs diffuse patterns
  • Regional tissue stress

Answers: "Is there a developing problem in this specific body region?"

Thermal baselines are athlete-specific and established directly within COMPASS across an initial series of scans. This is the only signal requiring internal baseline management.

The Founding Insight: These two questions are not the same question. A wearable cannot tell you that the right hamstring is inflamed. A thermogram cannot tell you whether the athlete slept well. The power of combining them is not that they overlap — it is that they do not.

Theoretical Foundation

Kalkhoven, Watsford & Impellizzeri (2021) demonstrated that training load measures cannot provide a quantitative indication of whether particular load progressions will increase or decrease injury risk at the tissue level — making the case for a mechanistic, tissue-specific layer above and beyond systemic load monitoring. COMPASS operationalises that argument directly: the Systemic Readiness Score captures the internal load domain (HRV, sleep, ACWR), while the Thermal Asymmetry Index adds the tissue-specific spatial resolution that no systemic metric can reach.

Kalkhoven JT, Watsford ML, Impellizzeri FM. A critique of the use and misuse of training load in team sports: towards a fuller understanding of the relationship between external and internal load. Sports Med. 2021;51(3):469–479. PMC7534945 ↗

Section 02

The COMPASS Framework

Plotting Systemic Readiness against Maximum Thermal Asymmetry creates a two-dimensional space in which every athlete occupies one of four clinically distinct zones — each carrying a genuinely different meaning and requiring a genuinely different clinical response.

IOC Load Consensus — Capacity Gap Model

The IOC Consensus on load management (Soligard et al. 2016, BJSM) defines injury risk as a function of the gap between an athlete's load and their capacity: when accumulated load exceeds the athlete's capacity to absorb it, injury risk rises. COMPASS zones map directly onto this load-capacity relationship — combining systemic capacity (Readiness Score) with local tissue capacity (Thermal Asymmetry Index) to characterise the precise nature of any capacity gap present.

Soligard T, et al. How much is too much? International Olympic Committee consensus statement on load in sport and risk of injury. Br J Sports Med. 2016;50(17):1030–1041.

Why the Deceptive Readiness Zone matters most: Injuries rarely happen to athletes who feel terrible. They happen most often to athletes who feel great, train hard, and cross a threshold that their tissue had been approaching invisibly for days or weeks. This zone is where thermography's early-warning capability saves careers.

3.1 — Scoring Equations

The three calculations that underpin the pipeline — T Score conversion, zone classification thresholds, and the COMPASS score formula.

① T Score — TAI → Thermal Risk (0–100)
Population methodT = min(100, TAI / 1.0 × 100)
SD baseline methodT = min(100, TAI_SD / 3.0 × 100)
Where TAI = max(region deltas) — the largest left/right temperature difference across all scanned zones. TAI_SD = TAI ÷ athlete's personal baseline SD. The SD baseline method is preferred for longitudinal monitoring.
② Zone Classification — R & T Thresholds
R ≥ 50 and T < 50 → Safe
R < 50 and T < 50 → Recovery
R ≥ 50 and T ≥ 50 → Deceptive
R < 50 and T ≥ 50 → Danger
③ COMPASS Score Formula
Abstract form — R_dist and T_dist are each signal's normalised distance from the zone boundary (0–1):
Safe / Recovery:   Score = floor + 24 × (0.5 × R_dist + 0.5 × T_dist)
Deceptive / Danger: Score = floor + 24 × (0.3 × R_dist + 0.7 × T_dist)
Per-zone expanded form:
Safe (76–100)76 + 24 × avg( (R−50)/50 , (50−T)/50 )
Recovery (51–75)51 + 24 × avg( R/50 , (50−T)/50 )
Deceptive (26–50)26 + 24 × ( 0.3×(R−50)/50 + 0.7×(100−T)/50 )
Danger (0–25)25 × ( 0.3×R/50 + 0.7×(100−T)/50 )
Zone floors: Safe 76 · Recovery 51 · Deceptive 26 · Danger 1. Each zone spans 25 points.
⚖️
Why Thermal Is Weighted 70% in Risk Zones

In the Deceptive and Danger zones, the thermal signal carries 70% of the within-zone score, versus 30% for readiness. This reflects the clinical asymmetry at the heart of the framework: when tissue stress is elevated, how the athlete feels is an unreliable guide. The score is deliberately pulled down by thermal risk even when readiness is high — ensuring an athlete who feels great but has significant asymmetry cannot score in the upper half of the Deceptive band.

Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1

Collect raw signals

Spike collects continuous sensor data and publishes one daily summary per athlete. Each day's output includes avg HRV resting HR sleep quality ACWR — these daily statistics are forwarded to the Spike API to generate the readiness score. The thermography camera captures a bilateral IR scan and returns TAI — the maximum contralateral delta across all scanned regions.

2

Compute R and T scores

The wearable API delivers a pre-normalised R score (0–100) — COMPASS does not perform any readiness modelling itself. The platform converts TAI to a T score (0–100) using either the population threshold method or the individual SD baseline method (preferred).

3

Classify zone via 2 × 2 matrix

R and T are each thresholded at 50. The combination of above/below determines which of the four zones applies: R ≥ 50, T < 50 → Safe; R < 50, T < 50 → Recovery; R ≥ 50, T ≥ 50 → Deceptive; R < 50, T ≥ 50 → Danger.

4

Apply zone-specific weighting

In Safe and Recovery zones the R and T contributions are equal (50/50). In Deceptive and Danger zones the thermal signal carries 70% of the within-zone score — reflecting the clinical reality that tissue stress is a more reliable injury predictor than perceived readiness when both signals conflict.

5

Calculate final COMPASS score

Each zone spans 25 points (floor to floor + 24). The weighted distances of R and T from their respective zone boundaries determine where within that 25-point band the athlete sits. The result is a single integer from 0–100 that encodes both zone identity and within-zone severity.

Steps 1–5 produce the COMPASS score and zone assignment. The pipeline then continues through an AI reasoning layer (Stage F) to practitioner outputs (Stage G) — detailed in Section 07. The full equations behind Steps 2–4 are in § 3.1.

Section 04

The Role of Personalisation

The readiness axis requires no baseline work from COMPASS — that is handled entirely by the wearable API provider. Personalisation within COMPASS is therefore focused exclusively on the thermal axis: establishing each athlete's individual thermal baseline so that asymmetry thresholds reflect what is elevated for them, not for the population.

⌚
Wearable Readiness: Zero Baseline Burden — Powered by Daily Spike Data

COMPASS receives a pre-computed, baseline-adjusted readiness score (0–100) directly from the Spike API. Spike collects raw sensor data continuously and publishes one daily summary per athlete — end-of-day averages for HRV, resting HR, sleep quality, and ACWR. These daily statistics are processed entirely by Spike: individual normalisation, HRV trend analysis, sleep scoring, and ACWR calculation all happen upstream. COMPASS consumes the output — no wearable data storage, baseline accumulation, or longitudinal modelling is required on the platform side.

Thermal Baseline Establishment

Thermography baselines must be built within COMPASS because they are institution- and equipment-specific. A meaningful personal thermal baseline requires a minimum series of scans captured under consistent conditions — see Section 08 (Standardised Scan Protocol) for the full specification.

04B.1 — Thermal Risk Score (T Score) Calculation

The Thermal Asymmetry Index (TAI) is converted to a normalised T Score (0–100) before entering the COMPASS fusion formula. This score represents the thermal risk axis of the two-axis matrix.

📐
TAI Definition

The Thermal Asymmetry Index is the maximum contralateral left-right temperature delta across all scanned body regions — i.e., the single worst-performing region drives the score. No averaging is applied; the highest asymmetry value is used as the input.

Phase Condition Formula Zone Crossing at T = 50
Population Baseline < 6 scans completed T = min(100, (TAI°C / 1.0) × 100) 0.5°C → T 50 · 1.0°C → T 100
Individual SD Baseline ≥ 6 scans completed T = min(100, (TAISD / 3.0) × 100) 1.5 SD → T 50 · 3.0 SD → T 100

Both formulas produce T = 50 at the Framework Alignment table's zone-crossing point (0.5°C / 1.5 SD). The transition at scan 6 is seamless: there is no step-change in classification because both scales are calibrated to the same physiological boundary. From scan 6 onward, the individual SD baseline takes precedence; the population formula is retired for that athlete.

🔄
Transition Behaviour at Scan 6

At exactly scan 6, both methods are computed in parallel and the individual SD result takes precedence. For athletes whose personal SD closely matches the population average (~0.27°C), the switch produces no perceptible change in zone assignment. For athletes with atypically high or low thermal variability, the individual baseline will produce a more accurate — and typically lower — T Score.

Section 06

Temporal Fusion Strategy

The two data streams operate on fundamentally different timescales. The framework explicitly models this asymmetry rather than engineering around it.

6.1 Thermal Data Gaps

Thermography is a periodic assessment — athletes are not scanned daily. Between scans, the system operates with a stale thermal signal: the most recent scan result persists as a contextual modifier, but its clinical weight diminishes the longer it goes unrefreshed. This section defines how COMPASS handles that gap: how long a thermal reading remains usable, how its influence is progressively reduced as the gap widens, and at what point the system treats the absence of fresh data as a risk condition in its own right.

📐
Modeling Assumption — Not Literature-Derived

The 7-day gap tolerance is a clinical approximation based on standard exponential decay, chosen for simplicity and auditability. No peer-reviewed study directly quantifies how long a thermographic asymmetry reading remains predictively valid after capture. This parameter should be treated as institution-adjustable: clinical leads are encouraged to revise it as longitudinal outcome data accumulates within their own cohort.

Gap Tolerance
7 days
Modeling assumption · Adjustable by clinical leads
⏰
Gap Threshold: 7 Days — Scoring Suspended

Once 7 days have elapsed since the last thermography scan, the thermal signal has degraded to 50% of its original clinical weight — below the minimum threshold for reliable zone classification. At this point the system does not attempt to extrapolate from stale data: thermal risk scoring is suspended and a scan-overdue flag is raised. Operating without a valid thermal input is itself treated as an elevated-risk state, since silent tissue stress accumulation cannot be excluded.

6.2 Handling Wearable Data Gaps

Spike publishes one daily summary per athlete — if a day's data is missing (device not worn, sync failure), no readiness score is available for that day. Unlike thermography (which decays gracefully), wearable data gaps require suspension — not downweighting. Yesterday's daily HRV average carries no reliable predictive weight for today's readiness.

✅
Gap 0–2 Days: Flagged Assessment

Zone classification proceeds using last known wearable values, visibly marked as "estimated — based on data from [date]." Practitioners receive a sync reminder but are not blocked from acting on the output.

🚫
Gap >2 Days: Assessment Suspended

No readiness score generated. No zone classification issued. If a valid thermal prior is available, a thermal-only advisory is produced. The daily output reads: "Zone assessment unavailable — wearable sync required. Last confirmed zone: [zone] on [date]."

Section 7.4

Return-to-Play & Injury Monitoring Protocol

COMPASS operates in two distinct clinical modes. Prevention mode (default) identifies hidden injury risk in active athletes. Monitoring mode activates after a confirmed injury: the same dual-signal architecture tracks tissue healing progress and loads tolerance through each rehabilitation phase, replacing subjective clinical feel with objective thermal-physiological gates.

The same thermal scan that detects hidden tissue stress before injury becomes the most objective tool available for governing the return-to-play decision. Individual baselines established during the prevention phase carry directly into monitoring mode — the data continuity is a core clinical advantage.

Why Phase-Gating Removes Guesswork: The most common driver of re-injury is premature return — an athlete who feels ready, whose wearable looks normal, but whose tissue has not completed repair. This is the same Deceptive Readiness state that causes first injuries. Phase gates anchored to thermal trajectory and individual baseline make the progression decision objective and auditable.

Section 7.4.3

Thermal Trajectory as Objective Progression Gate

In monitoring mode, the T Score is the primary gate for phase progression. A single reading is insufficient — what matters is the trajectory across successive scans under incrementally increasing load.

  • Improving trajectory (T Score rising across scans): Load is within tissue repair capacity — safe to maintain or progress.
  • Plateau (T Score stable across ≥2 scans at same load level): Tissue tolerance ceiling reached at current load — hold phase, do not progress.
  • Worsening trajectory (T Score declining despite stable or reduced load): Current rehabilitation load is exceeding tissue repair capacity — regress one phase and reassess.
  • RTP clearance: Not a single reading but a confirmed trend — thermal asymmetry within 0.5 SD of individual baseline across ≥3 consecutive scans with progressive load.
Section 7.4.4

Contralateral Limb Monitoring

Injured athletes compensate by redistributing load to the contralateral (uninjured) limb. COMPASS bilateral scanning tracks both limbs simultaneously throughout rehabilitation. The contralateral limb requires its own thermal trajectory assessment — independent of the injury site.

⚠️ Contralateral Alert

Rising thermal asymmetry on the healthy limb during rehabilitation signals compensatory overload. The contralateral limb is entering a Deceptive Readiness state — the athlete cannot perceive this stress.

Action: Load redistribution Physio: Mandatory

🟢 Bilateral Symmetry Restored

Both the injured site and the contralateral limb showing thermal asymmetry within 0.5 SD of individual baseline is the full-body clearance condition. Unilateral clearance alone is insufficient.

Both limbs: ≤0.5 SD baseline Status: RTP eligible

Seated rest causes vascular compression of the posterior thigh against the chair, suppressing hamstring perfusion and producing thermal asymmetry artefacts. Pre-Scan Restrictions

Athletes must observe the following restrictions prior to each scan session. Document compliance as part of the session record.

Factor Minimum Restriction Window Rationale
Vigorous exercise (training, match play) ≥ 24 hours Exercise-induced skin temperature changes persist for hours. Reliable asymmetry readings require full thermoregulatory recovery. See Timing section below.

Scan Timing Relative to Exercise

The preferred scan window is before that day's training session. Pre-training scanning eliminates exercise-induced thermal artefacts entirely, produces the most stable baseline readings, and ensures scans are not lost to scheduling conflicts after a session. Always aim to scan at the same time of day relative to training.

📋
Post-Exercise Thermal Response

Skin temperature does not simply remain elevated after exercise. It follows a biphasic pattern: an initial drop in the first 0–15 minutes (vasoconstriction during cool-down), followed by a prolonged rebound rise that can persist for several hours as inflammatory and perfusion responses evolve. This dynamic, non-monotonic response means there is no reliable post-exercise window for valid asymmetry assessment below 24 hours. Post-exercise scans should be avoided for diagnostic purposes. (Masur et al., Frontiers systematic review, 2024)

Skin temperature follows a circadian rhythm, rising through the morning and plateauing in the afternoon. The difference between peak and trough is approximately 1°C — more than twice the COMPASS alert threshold. Intra-athlete comparisons must always be made between scans taken at the same time of day. (ScienceDirect, 2015)

If your programme's schedule requires morning scanning, establish the athlete's baseline entirely within morning sessions.

Normative thermal asymmetry dataset from 950 healthy high-performance athletes without pain, injury, or influence factors. 1.8 — SD Classification Framework in Sports Monitoring

Evidence supporting the use of standard deviation tiers — rather than fixed absolute values — as the authoritative classification system once an individual baseline is established. These studies underpin the COMPASS 1.5 SD alert threshold and the broader SD tier framework in Section 05B.

Strong

Establishes the widely-adopted SD tier framework for individual monitoring in sport: <0.5 SD = within normal variation; 1.0 SD = meaningful departure (training modification flag); 1.5 SD = clinically important; ≥2.0 SD = extreme deviation requiring clinical review. Applied here to thermography because the same intra-individual departure logic applies: the question is not "how far from population average?" but "how far from this athlete's personal norm?" Directly supports the 1.0 / 1.5 / 2.0 SD tier boundaries used in Section 05B.
Strong

British Journal of Sports Medicine, 50(3):192–200
Intra-individual variability in HRV, sleep, and wellness scores is more sensitive to training load and fatigue than absolute values. Individual-referenced thresholds outperform population-referenced thresholds across all wellness biomarkers. Below 1 SD from personal baseline = normal fluctuation; above 1.5 SD = clinically important departure. Directly validates the principle that individual SD thresholds supersede population °C thresholds — the foundational logic of the Section 05B framework.

Claudino et al. (2024) — Individual Baselines in ML Models

British Journal of Sports Medicine
Machine learning models trained on individual athlete baselines significantly outperformed models trained on group norms for injury risk prediction (AUC 0.84 vs. 0.61). 28–35 days of training data required for reliable individual baseline establishment (consistent with the 4–6 scan, 3–5 week COMPASS onboarding protocol). Quantifies the performance gap between individual and population reference frames: the SD-based individual framework is not just conceptually superior — it is measurably more predictive.

1.9 — Thermal Asymmetry Risk Stratification Studies

Studies directly establishing which absolute asymmetry values (°C) correspond to clinically meaningful injury risk levels. These anchor the Moderate and High population-based tiers where the literature provides most specific threshold evidence.

Moderate

Marins, J.C.B. et al. (2014)

International Journal of Morphology, 32(3):769–776
Thermal body pattern study in healthy Brazilian adults establishing region-specific normative asymmetry values and risk gradients. Asymmetry ≥ 0.3°C warrants clinical attention in major muscle groups. Values approaching 0.4°C indicate meaningfully elevated injury risk. Recommended for clinical investigation and contextualisation with load data when exceeded. Anchors the lower boundary of the Moderate tier (0.30°C) and confirms the transition zone toward the High tier prevention threshold approaching 0.4–0.5°C.
Strong

Gómez-Carmona et al. (2020) — 0.5°C Clinical Threshold

Journal of Sport Rehabilitation, 29(8):1222–1227
Asymmetry >0.5°C identified 83% of subsequent soft tissue injuries before symptom onset (n=24 professional players). Detection occurred an average 8.2 days prior to clinical presentation. Daily thermography protocol reduced injuries from 0.63 to 0.25 per player, and days lost from 156 to 14. Definitively establishes 0.5°C as the lower boundary of the High tier — and confirms that the corresponding SD value (≈ 1.85 SD) sits firmly within the 1.5–1.99 SD COMPASS alert band, validating the calibration between both frameworks.
Moderate

Arnaiz-Lastras et al. (2019) — 0.4°C Prevention Protocol

PMC — Pilot Study (Complementary Screening)
Twice-weekly thermography (48 hours post-match) in professional soccer. Prevention protocol triggered when asymmetry exceeded 0.4°C. Muscle injuries reduced from 11 to 4 over the monitored period — a 64% reduction. Establishes 0.4°C as an operational prevention threshold. Critically, 0.4°C ≈ 1.5 SD for major muscle groups, confirming that the COMPASS 1.5 SD alert threshold and this literature-based 0.4°C threshold are drawing the line at the same physiological signal.

1.10 — Training Load Theory & the Capacity Gap Model

The theoretical underpinning of COMPASS's dual-axis design: why systemic load monitoring alone is insufficient for tissue-level injury risk detection, and how the IOC load-capacity model maps onto the four COMPASS zones.

Strong

Kalkhoven, J.T., Watsford, M.L., & Impellizzeri, F.M. (2021)

Sports Medicine, 51(3):469–479 · PMC7534945 ↗
Demonstrated that training load measures — whether external (GPS, ACWR) or internal (HRV, RPE) — cannot provide a quantitative indication of whether particular load progressions will increase or decrease injury risk at the tissue level. Argued that a mechanistic, tissue-specific layer is required above and beyond systemic load monitoring. Directly motivates the COMPASS dual-axis architecture: the Thermal Asymmetry Index provides the spatially-resolved tissue signal that no systemic metric can reach, operationalising the theoretical gap this paper identifies.
Strong

Soligard, T. et al. (2016) — IOC Consensus on Load in Sport

British Journal of Sports Medicine, 50(17):1030–1041
International Olympic Committee consensus statement defining injury risk as a function of the gap between an athlete's accumulated load and their capacity to absorb it: when load exceeds capacity, injury risk rises. Established the load-capacity framework as the theoretical lens for interpreting all monitoring data. COMPASS zones map directly onto this model — Safe (no gap), Recovery (systemic capacity eroded), Deceptive Readiness (hidden local-tissue capacity gap invisible to systemic metrics), Danger (critical dual-axis gap) — providing a principled clinical interpretation for each zone beyond the score label alone.
